# Final Deliverable Brief

## IS117 — Web Presence Finals

**During the final exam on Zoom during the scheduled exam period , you will present two live public products.**

---

## Product 1 — Your Portfolio Site

### What it is

A signal-first personal portfolio that makes you legible, credible, and actionable to one specific audience.

This is not a collection of everything you have done. It is a designed argument for what you offer and who should care.

### What it must do

1. Communicate one clear archetype on the first read — before anyone reads a word
2. State a specific professional promise on the first screen
3. Show real proof near the promise — not buried, not vague
4. Give the visitor a clear next step
5. Feel like one person made it with intention, not like a template with your name on it

### What you will submit

- A live URL
- Your completed workbook Signal Brief (Section 2A) — this is your stated intention
- Your most important agent spec from the three-week build process
- A one-paragraph reflection: what did you ask your agents to do, and how did the output change as your briefs got clearer?

### How it will be reviewed

You will pull up the live site. Someone in the room will describe what they understand about you from a 5-second look. We compare that to your signal brief.

The gap between intention and outcome is the design problem.

## Product 2 — Your Museum Site

### What it is

A curated, designed public experience centered on a subject you chose and built using AI-agent orchestration with a spec and sprint process.

This is not an encyclopedia entry or a personal blog. It is a considered editorial product that applies design and attention principles to make a subject legible and worth spending time with.

### What it must do

1. Communicate the archetype of the subject — the register that fits what the subject actually is — on the first read
2. Show a site promise that captures why this subject deserves a serious public experience
3. Demonstrate real curatorial judgment — not just information, but information shaped by a point of view
4. Apply attention and design principles: clear hierarchy, proof of editorial care, a page that earns the visit
5. Read like a site that knows what it is and why it was made

### What you will submit

- A live URL
- Your completed workbook Signal Brief (Section 2B) — your stated intention for the subject and visitor
- One example of an agent spec you wrote and the output it produced
- A one-paragraph reflection: what design and attention principle had the most visible effect on the final site?

### How it will be reviewed

You will pull up the live site. Someone in the room will describe the subject, the feeling, and the apparent editorial intelligence of the site after a 30-second look. We compare that to your signal brief.

We will also ask: could you describe what you told your agent? A strong answer here shows command of the orchestration process, not just the output.

## What strong work looks like

Strong work does not mean perfect visual execution.

Strong work means:

1. The intention is clear and stated (your signal brief)
2. The product is honest about what it is and who it is for
3. The proof is real — there are receipts, not just claims
4. The agent specs got more precise over three weeks, not more vague
5. The student can explain the decisions, not just show the output

A site with a clear brief, visible proof, and a coherent first read is stronger than a beautiful site with no argument behind it.

---

## Common failure modes

**For the portfolio:**
- Mixing archetypes — the first read communicates three different personalities
- Hiding the best work at the bottom
- A headline that says nothing specific about the person or the promise
- A CTA that goes to "contact me" with no context for why someone should

**For the museum site:**
- Building a topic summary instead of a curated experience
- Design that performs complexity without revealing the subject
- Navigation that reflects the builder's research process, not the visitor's reading path
- No visible editorial judgment — the site could have been auto-generated

---

## One reminder

The goal of the identity and design system is not to make your page look impressive.

It is to make the right person understand you clearly, trust what they see, and know what to do next.

A page that does those three things for one specific person is a better portfolio than a page that tries to impress everyone and lands nowhere.
