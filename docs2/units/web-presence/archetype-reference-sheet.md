# Archetype Reference Sheet

## The 12 Archetypes — 4 Families

Use this alongside your workbook. Read through all 12 before making a choice.

Each archetype includes: the core promise it makes, what the page feels like before anyone reads it, the gift when used well, and the trap when it fails.

---

## Family 1 — Yearning for Paradise
### Archetypes: Innocent · Explorer · Sage

These archetypes connect the visitor to something better — clarity, discovery, or understanding. They work through openness rather than drama.

---

### Innocent

**Core promise:** This will be clear, honest, and safe.

**First-read feel:** Clean, minimal, warm, optimistic. Low visual noise. Feels approachable and undefensive.

**Gift:** Immediate trust. Visitors do not feel sold to. The page feels like it has nothing to hide.

**Trap:** Can read as naive, lightweight, or without depth. Works poorly if the work itself is complex or demands authority.

**Fits:** First work, educational content, tools designed for accessibility, anything where belonging matters more than expertise.

---

### Explorer

**Core promise:** This will take you somewhere new.

**First-read feel:** Open, dynamic, movement-oriented. Visual breadth rather than depth. Feels like a beginning, not a conclusion.

**Gift:** Creates curiosity. The visitor wants to follow. Natural fit for research, investigation, and forward-looking work.

**Trap:** Can feel unfocused or restless. If the page does not eventually land somewhere, the energy reads as wandering.

**Fits:** Discovery-oriented work, research, emerging areas, building something that does not look like what came before.

---

### Sage

**Core promise:** This will be accurate, grounded, and worth trusting.

**First-read feel:** Structured, measured, evidence-aware. Strong typographic hierarchy. Claims feel backed before they are stated.

**Gift:** Deep trust. Visitors believe the work is real because the page communicates method and discipline.

**Trap:** Can feel cold, distant, or hard to enter. If there is no human warmth anywhere, the page becomes intimidating rather than authoritative.

**Fits:** Technical expertise, research-heavy work, educational systems, consulting, anything where credibility is the main argument.

---

## Family 2 — Leaving a Thumbprint on the World
### Archetypes: Hero · Outlaw · Magician

These archetypes connect the visitor to change, challenge, or transformation. They work through energy and assertion.

---

### Hero

**Core promise:** This will show you what is possible with effort and skill.

**First-read feel:** Forward-moving, goal-oriented, high contrast, decisive. Feels like someone in motion.

**Gift:** Inspiration. The visitor wants to be part of something. Strong for achievement-oriented work and competitive fields.

**Trap:** Can feel aggressive, exhausting, or self-congratulatory. Works poorly if the proof does not match the energy.

**Fits:** Achievement-oriented portfolios, results-driven work, competitive professional contexts, building toward a defined goal.

---

### Outlaw

**Core promise:** This challenges what you assumed was fixed.

**First-read feel:** High contrast, rule-bending, surprising. Edges visible. Feels deliberately different from the category default.

**Gift:** Sharp differentiation. No one confuses this with similar work. The visitor notices immediately that something different is happening.

**Trap:** Can alienate. The edge must serve an idea, not perform rebellion for its own sake. Requires real substance behind the contrast.

**Fits:** Counterintuitive work, critique, disruption, anything that argues against a category default. Works poorly as an affectation.

---

### Magician

**Core promise:** This will change how you see something.

**First-read feel:** Transformation-oriented, system-aware, before-and-after logic. Feels like entering a different understanding.

**Gift:** Fascination. The visitor is pulled into a journey that shifts their perspective. Strong for complex or emerging subjects.

**Trap:** Can read as vague or grandiose without concrete proof. Transformation claims require visible evidence of what actually changed.

**Fits:** Creative process work, AI and emerging technology, transformation-heavy subjects, anything where the point is a shift in understanding.

---

## Family 3 — No Man is an Island
### Archetypes: Regular Guy/Gal · Lover · Jester

These archetypes connect the visitor through belonging, taste, or pleasure. They work through relatability rather than authority.

---

### Regular Guy/Gal (Everyman)

**Core promise:** This is for someone like you.

**First-read feel:** Plain language, approachable imagery, no performance. Feels unpretentious and honest.

**Gift:** Immediate belonging. Nobody feels excluded. Works well when the audience is broad and the work is practical.

**Trap:** Can disappear into the background. Very hard to remember or recommend. Generic execution kills this archetype.

**Fits:** Community-focused work, practical tools, accessible services, anything where inclusion is more important than distinction.

---

### Lover

**Core promise:** This is for someone who cares deeply about quality.

**First-read feel:** Sensory, beautiful, intimate, specific. Feels attended to rather than assembled. Details matter visibly.

**Gift:** Emotional resonance. The visitor feels seen and understood. Works well when taste and craft are central arguments.

**Trap:** Can feel indulgent or self-conscious without a clear purpose behind the beauty. Requires substance to avoid feeling decorative.

**Fits:** Craft-focused work, design, food, culture, any domain where the experience and quality of attention is itself the argument.

---

### Jester

**Core promise:** This will be smart and probably more interesting than it looks.

**First-read feel:** Light, playful, unexpected humor, a sense of knowing the rules and bending them on purpose.

**Gift:** Disarming. Gets past defenses faster than serious registers. Visitors drop their guard.

**Trap:** Can undermine trust in serious work. Very hard to maintain past the first screen. Humor that does not serve an idea becomes noise.

**Fits:** Creative work that does not take itself too seriously, culture critique, light satire, anything where wit is itself evidence of intelligence.

---

## Family 4 — Providing Structure to the World
### Archetypes: Caregiver · Creator · Ruler

These archetypes connect the visitor to care, craft, or order. They work through reliability and competence.

---

### Caregiver

**Core promise:** This is here for you.

**First-read feel:** Warm, supportive, accessible, human imagery. Feels like the page anticipates your needs and answers them.

**Gift:** Emotional safety. The visitor feels taken care of rather than assessed. Removes friction from trust.

**Trap:** Can feel paternalistic or overprotective. Still requires real proof — warmth without substance is credibility-destroying.

**Fits:** Support services, education, healthcare, mentorship, anything where the relationship between provider and person is the primary value.

---

### Creator

**Core promise:** This is the result of genuine making.

**First-read feel:** Craft-visible, process-aware, originality in the details. The work feels made rather than assembled.

**Gift:** Integrity. The visitor sees genuine skill at work. Works well when the originality of the thinking is itself the argument.

**Trap:** Can become precious or portfolio-for-portfolio's-sake. The craft must serve communication, not replace it.

**Fits:** Design work, engineering systems, original research, anything where the visible quality of making is a primary signal of value.

---

### Ruler

**Core promise:** This is the standard.

**First-read feel:** Premium spacing, controlled order, restrained palette, authority in the structure itself. Operates from a position of confidence.

**Gift:** Immediate credibility. Reads like the category leader. Works well in contexts where authority is expected.

**Trap:** Can feel inaccessible, exclusionary, or defensive. Fails when the work does not actually warrant the register.

**Fits:** Senior-level work, executive contexts, organizational design, governance systems, anything where the primary argument is that the person or work sets the standard.

---

## Quick comparison by axis

| If the page should feel... | Consider |
| --- | --- |
| Trustworthy, grounded, methodical | Sage, Ruler |
| Warm, approachable, human | Caregiver, Everyman, Innocent |
| Exciting, forward-moving, ambitious | Hero, Explorer |
| Surprising, defiant, counterintuitive | Outlaw, Jester |
| Beautiful, specific, craft-forward | Lover, Creator |
| Transformative, visionary, system-aware | Magician |

---

## A note on mixing

You will be tempted to pick two or three. Do not.

A page can have secondary traits — a Sage-primary with slight Creator warmth, for example. But if the first read does not communicate one clear archetype, it communicates none. The secondary trait should be invisible on the first read and become visible only on closer attention.

The test: show the page to someone for five seconds. Ask them to describe the feeling. If they say two different archetypes, the work is not done.
