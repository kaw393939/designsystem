You have already been introduced to this chain. Here it is compressed to a memory aid.

```
Need → Signal → Style → Proof → CTA → Publish → Repeat
```

| Step | Question |
| --- | --- |

| Proof | What evidence sits near the promise? |

| Publish | Where does this page go in the world? |

In Sessions 1 and 2, you are working on Need, Signal, Style, and Proof for both products. The rest will follow from those decisions.

## Section 1 — The Archetype Map

Archetypes are not personality types. They are public meaning systems — the interpretive frame a page creates before anyone reads a word.

A page communicates its archetype through headline tone, imagery, layout density, proof style, and the language of its call to action. The visitor reads the frame before they read the content. If the frame is mixed or absent, they leave faster.

### Museum site archetype — a different question

For your museum site, the archetype question is different.

You are not deciding who you are. You are deciding **what register your subject demands from a visitor.**

Think about the difference between walking through a natural history museum, a contemporary art gallery, and a jazz club. The subject shapes the experience. A site about quantum computing feels different from a site about street food culture, and that difference is not random — it is the subject itself signaling what kind of attention it deserves.

The test is this: if a visitor walks into a real physical exhibit on your topic, what does it feel like to move through it? What would feel *wrong* if the exhibit had a completely different register?

Some subjects call for Sage — evidence-first, measured, trustworthy. Some call for Explorer — discovery-oriented, open-ended, movement forward. Some call for Magician — the subject transforms how you see something. Some call for Creator — the craft and process of making is itself the story.

Your job is to find the archetypal register your subject actually wants, not the one that sounded interesting when you started.

## Section 3 — The First-Read Check

Before you audit your pages, here are the three principles that govern what happens in the first 5 seconds of a visit.

---

**Principle 1: The first read happens before reading begins.**

Visitors do not start by reading your headline. They start by reading the visual field — the weight of your typography, the dominant image, the density of the layout, the color temperature. Their brain classifies the page (trustworthy / suspicious, complex / simple, personal / institutional) before a single sentence is processed.

This means your archetype has to survive in the visual field before anyone reads a word.

**Principle 2: Hierarchy is trust.**

A clear visual hierarchy communicates that the page has a point of view — that someone made decisions about what matters. A flat or noisy hierarchy communicates the opposite. Trust comes from structure, not from attractive styling.

**Principle 3: Proof must be close to the promise.**

If your headline makes a claim and your evidence is three scrolls away, the visitor disappears before the evidence appears. Proof and promise belong near each other. This is structural, not stylistic.

Proof is not a section at the bottom. It is the evidence that sits closest to the claim.

A proof block is a compact, honest demonstration that the promise made in Section 2 is real. It is not a testimonial or a list of tools. It is a receipt.
