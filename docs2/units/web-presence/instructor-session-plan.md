# Instructor Session Plan

## IS117 — Web Presence Workshop
## Sessions 1 and 2 — Facilitator Notes

---

## Overview

Two 80-minute sessions designed to move students from abstract familiarity with archetypes and design principles to concrete signal decisions for their two finals: the portfolio site and the museum site.

Students have already been introduced to archetypes and design styles. These sessions are not re-introduction — they are decision-forcing. The workbook is the primary vehicle. Your job is to keep the room moving, surface interesting tensions for whole-class discussion, and prevent students from escaping into abstraction.

The key pedagogical insight for this class: students built their museum sites using AI-agent orchestration, spec-first. The signal decisions they make in these sessions become the founding specifications they give their agents over the next three weeks. Make this connection explicit early. It is the most concrete reason to do this work carefully.

---

## Session 1 — Signal and Archetype

**Timestamp: 8:30–9:50**

---

### 8:30–8:38 — Frame the two finals (8 min)

Open with the deliverable brief. Put the two products in front of students before touching archetypes.

Talking points:
- "Two finals. Two different signal problems. Both benefit from the same decision you make today."
- "Your portfolio is a signal about you. Your museum site is a signal about a subject. Same framework, different application."
- "The decisions you make in the next 80 minutes become the specs you write for your agents. Vague decisions produce vague output. Precise decisions produce sites that actually do something."

Do not spend more than 8 minutes here. The frame is set; move to the archetypes.

---

### 8:38–8:55 — Archetype Exploration (17 min)

Students use the archetype reference sheet alongside Section 1 of the workbook.

Sequence:
1. Give students 5–6 minutes to read through all 12 independently. No talking.
2. Ask them to cross out archetypes that feel wrong for each product. Circle options for portfolio. Star options for museum site.
3. Then open briefly: "What did you cross out first and why?" Take 2–3 responses. This surfaces the most useful distinctions without becoming a lecture.

Watch for:
- Students who circled more than 3 for either product — they have not committed to a lens yet. Prompt: "If you had to eliminate one more, which goes?"
- Students who circled the same archetype for both products — this is possible but worth interrogating. Prompt: "Does your subject's register actually match how you want to present yourself?"
- Students attracted to Outlaw or Jester — ask what the trap is before they commit. These archetypes require more discipline than they appear to.

Do not let this become a personality test conversation. Redirect: "The question is not which archetype describes you as a person. It is which archetype the page needs to communicate."

---

### 8:55–9:15 — Portfolio Signal Brief (Section 2A) (20 min)

Students work individually. You circulate.

The most common problem: the audience prompt produces generic answers ("any employer in tech"). When you see this, stop the student and ask: "What city? What size company? What are they worried about the week they look at your page?" Push toward specificity.

The agent spec note is new territory for most students. Help them understand the translation: the decisions in the brief become the language of a prompt. If they say their portfolio should feel like Sage, ask: "What would you tell your agent about the headline? The imagery? The proof placement?" That is the spec.

If students finish early, ask them to reread their one-sentence professional promise and apply the test: "Can someone who has never met you understand what you do, who you do it for, and why you are good at it from this one sentence?" Most first drafts fail this test.

---

### 9:15–9:35 — Museum Site Signal Brief (Section 2B) (20 min)

Before students open to Section 2B, spend 2–3 minutes framing the editorial archetype concept. This is the key teaching moment for this section.

Say something like:

> "For your museum site, the archetype question is different from your portfolio. You are not deciding who you are. You are deciding what register your subject demands from a visitor. Think about the difference between walking into a natural history museum and a contemporary art gallery. The subject shapes the experience. A site about jazz should feel different from a site about supply chain logistics — not because of your personal taste, but because the subjects themselves have different registers. Your job is to find the archetype that fits the subject, not the one you happen to like."

Then send them to Section 2B. Circulate. Watch for:

- Students defaulting to the same archetype as their portfolio — sometimes right, often not thought through. Prompt: "Does the subject's tone actually match yours?"
- Students writing very generic site promises ("a place to learn about X"). Push toward: "What does a visitor understand or feel after 10 seconds that they did not before?"
- Students with museum sites on topics that have a clear emotional register (war, music, civil rights, sports) — these are usually easier because the subject demands something strongly. Help them name it.

The agent spec note for the museum site should be the most specific thing in this section. If a student writes a vague spec, ask: "If you sent this to your agent right now, what would it produce?" Usually that question reveals the vagueness.

---

### 9:35–9:48 — Pair Review (Section 2C) and share out (13 min)

Students exchange workbooks with one partner. They read the two briefs and respond verbally or in writing.

After 5–6 minutes of pairs, pull 2–3 examples for whole-class discussion. Ask for volunteers or pick students whose work has interesting tensions.

Good whole-class prompts:
- "Can someone read this brief back and tell us what kind of page they expect to see?" (Read the brief aloud, get a response from someone who has not seen the student's site)
- "Where does the archetype choice feel surprising given the subject?"
- "What is the difference between this portfolio brief and this museum site brief for the same student — and does that difference make sense?"

---

### 9:48–9:50 — Wrap (2 min)

Assign: for Session 2, pull up both sites before class. Have screenshots ready if the sites are not live.

"Next session we audit what you actually have against these briefs. The gap is the design problem."

---

## Session 2 — Apply, Audit, and Plan

**Timestamp: 8:30–9:50**

---

### 8:30–8:40 — First-read principles (Section 3) (10 min)

This is a reminder, not a lecture. Students have heard these ideas. You are giving them a diagnostic lens before they audit their pages.

Cover the three principles from Section 3 quickly:
1. The first read happens before reading begins (visual field, not text)
2. Hierarchy communicates that someone made decisions — and that trust transfers to the work
3. Proof must be close to the promise

You can make this concrete with one fast classroom demonstration: open a site you like (not a student's). Cover the text with your hand. Ask: "What archetype does this feel like from the visual field alone?" Then reveal the copy. Does it match?

Do not spend more than 10 minutes here. The audit is where the learning happens.

---

### 8:40–8:58 — Portfolio Audit (Section 4A) (18 min)

Students open their portfolio sites and work through the audit table. Circulate.

What to look for:
- Students who audit everything as "fine" — this usually means they are not applying the brief. Prompt: "Your brief says Sage. Does this headline sound Sage or does it sound like something else?"
- Students who audit harshly — good. Help them prioritize. "Which one change would raise your score the most?"
- The agent spec box after the audit is the most important part. If a student cannot write a spec for the change they need to make, they have not decided what they want. Work through this with them.

---

### 8:58–9:16 — Museum Site Audit (Section 4B) (18 min)

Same structure as portfolio audit. Museum sites will show more variance because students have had different design intentions going in.

Watch for:
- Sites that look attractive but have no clear editorial logic — the design is covering the absence of curation. Prompt: "If someone asked you what your site has that Wikipedia does not, what would you say?"
- Sites with strong content but weak hierarchy — the curatorial intelligence is real but not visible on first read. Good problem to have; the fix is structural.
- "What would you spec differently" is often the most revealing question. If a student cannot answer it, they do not yet have a clear picture of the gap.

---

### 9:16–9:28 — Proof Blocks (Section 5) (12 min)

Students draft one proof block for each product.

Portfolio proof: the most common failure is writing a description of the project rather than a proof of capability. Stop students who are summarizing. "What changed because of this? What is the result a visitor can see or verify?"

Museum site proof: emphasize that curatorial quality is the argument here. The proof that the site is well-made is the combination of: clear sourcing, editorial voice (not just aggregation), design serving the subject.

---

### 9:28–9:40 — 3-Week Build Plan (Section 6) (12 min)

Students fill in the table. Be realistic with them. Three weeks is not a lot of time.

The column "What you are speccing for your agents" is the most future-facing. Students who have a spec in mind for each week have a real plan. Students who write "work on the homepage" do not.

Push toward specificity: "So in Week 1, what one agent prompt are you writing? What do you expect back?"

---

### 9:40–9:50 — Whole-class review (10 min)

Pull 2–3 students. For each:
1. They pull up the live site
2. Someone else describes what they see in 30 seconds WITHOUT being told what the student intended
3. Student reads their signal brief
4. Room discusses the gap

This is the most useful 10-minute teaching moment in the two sessions. Do not skip it.

Good facilitation prompts:
- "What archetype did you read? What in the page made you say that?"
- "What would you change in the spec if you were the agent?"
- "If this is the gap — what is the one-sentence fix the student needs to brief?"

End by confirming the studio review format and what students should bring to each class session going forward.

---

## What to watch for across both sessions

**Students who are performing rather than deciding.** They pick an archetype that sounds good rather than one that fits the actual work. The tell: they cannot explain the archetype demand (what headline style, what proof style, what CTA style) — they can only name it.

**Students who want to skip the museum site framing.** The museum site feels like "already done" to some students. Push back: the build process is not done just because the site exists. The signal brief is the evaluation framework. If they cannot state the site's archetype and promise, they cannot finish the site well.

**Students who write agent specs that are still vague.** The spec quality reflects the clarity of the decision. If the spec is vague, the decision is vague. Work the decision until the spec becomes specific.

**Students who over-commit to polish over argument.** A beautifully styled site with no clear promise is not a strong final. Redirect toward substance: what does the page argue?

## Connection to the degree

If you have time during either session, note the larger context: this is the identity and signal layer of the formation model. Reading yourself and your subject clearly, writing a precise enough spec to brief an agent, building toward visible proof — these are not just portfolio tasks. They are the core of what the degree is trying to form. Students who can do this in IS117 are practicing skills they will use in every subsequent course.
