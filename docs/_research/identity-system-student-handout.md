# Identity Portfolio Student Handout

## What this is

Use this when your portfolio looks polished but still says almost nothing.

This handout is the shortest version of the system.

It is not about inventing a fake brand. It is about making it easier for the right person to understand you, trust your work, and know what to do next.

## The seven-part memory aid

1. Need
2. Signal
3. Style
4. Proof
5. CTA
6. Publish
7. Repeat

If you forget everything else, remember that chain.

## What each step means

### Need

What does your audience actually care about or worry about?

Do not start with tools. Start with the tension.

### Signal

What should someone understand about you in one quick read?

Pick one primary archetype and one plain-language promise.

### Style

What should the page feel like in the first second?

Use layout, type, images, and contrast to make the signal visible fast.

### Proof

What artifact proves the claim fastest?

Put the receipt near the promise.

### CTA

What should the reader do next?

Make the next step clear, small, and real.

### Publish

Where will this page show up outside the browser?

Think posts, conversations, follow-ups, and meetups.

### Repeat

What did people understand first?

What did they miss?

That answer shapes the next version.

## The one-screen checklist

Before you ship, ask:

1. Can a stranger name the signal after a quick scan?
2. Is the best proof visible early enough?
3. Does the page feel like one person, not three mixed together?
4. Is the next step obvious?
5. Do I know where I will share this in public?

## Common mistakes

1. Trying to sound impressive before sounding clear.
2. Mixing multiple archetypes on the first screen.
3. Using design trends that do not support the signal.
4. Hiding the proof under too much explanation.
5. Treating publishing like a bonus round instead of part of the assignment.

## First build session

If you only have one work session, do this:

1. Write one sentence about who the page is for.
2. Write one sentence about what you want them to understand about you.
3. Pick one image, artifact, or screenshot that proves it.
4. Write one CTA.
5. Show the page to one person and ask what they remember.

## Final reminder

You do not need a perfect portfolio.

You need a page that gives the right person a clear read, real proof, and a reason to respond.
