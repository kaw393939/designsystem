# BSEAI IA QA Review Panel

## Status

- Status: active review-planning brief
- Scope: expert lenses, review sequence, and sign-off criteria for QAing the BSEAI information architecture before content and media production
- Parent docs: `docs/_ux/bseai-site-information-architecture.md`, `docs/_ux/is117-online-presence-course-architecture.md`, `docs/_projects/bseaid-degree/degree-positioning.md`, `docs/_projects/bseaid-degree/bseai-media-and-visualization-production-plan.md`

## Purpose

Define which expert lenses should review the IA so it becomes a reliable source document for:

1. route and module creation
2. media production
3. content authoring
4. research-asset reuse
5. exemplar planning

This is not a generic stakeholder list.

It is the minimum review structure needed to stop the IA from drifting into one of its likely failure states:

1. a research archive instead of a curated experience
2. a content system with weak teaching structure
3. a beautiful media plan detached from real source material
4. a coherent doctrine that is too vague to build

## Working recommendation

Use two layers of QA.

### Required review layer

These reviewers should be treated as required.

### Advisory review layer

These reviewers are not always needed in the first pass, but they strengthen realism, credibility, and implementation quality.

If staffing is limited, one person can cover more than one lens.

The important rule is that every lens gets covered.

## Core sign-off panel

### 1. Information architect and content strategist

Primary job:
Check whether the IA is structurally legible and whether the route, room, and module hierarchy actually works.

Key questions:

1. does every top-level route have one clear job
2. are route names and groupings understandable to non-authors
3. is the atlas, gallery, studio, and course-wrapper distinction clear enough to prevent duplication
4. does the IA help authors know where a new page or asset belongs

What this reviewer should approve:

1. route tree
2. page-job logic
3. naming and taxonomy
4. primary and secondary navigation rules

### 2. Instructional designer and curriculum architect

Primary job:
Check whether the IA works as a teaching system rather than just a content map.

Key questions:

1. are learning outcomes visible and aligned
2. do modules lead to artifacts rather than only understanding
3. can course wrappers reuse canonical modules cleanly
4. does the sequence support face-to-face, hybrid, and online teaching

What this reviewer should approve:

1. module shell
2. outcome map
3. wrapper structure
4. assessment and interaction logic

### 3. Curator and editorial experience director

Primary job:
Check whether the site actually behaves like a curated museum-school experience instead of a dump of strong ideas.

Key questions:

1. does each room have a real interpretive arc
2. are the chosen objects, examples, quote rails, and evidence cards selective enough
3. are visitors being moved from interpretation into action
4. does the atlas feel curated or merely indexed

What this reviewer should approve:

1. room concept and opening-panel logic
2. exhibit grammar
3. object and case-card selection rules
4. next-move handoff between rooms, studio, and examples

### 4. Brand and identity-systems strategist

Primary job:
Check whether the identity and archetype layer is being used as a coherence system rather than personality entertainment.

Key questions:

1. are archetypes positioned as public meaning systems rather than diagnoses
2. does each archetype room include tone, proof, CTA, gift, and trap logic
3. is the Hero and the Outlaw cluster structure being used well
4. does the system force real positioning choices instead of adjective soup

What this reviewer should approve:

1. archetype atlas structure
2. compare logic for commonly confused archetypes
3. proof-style and CTA-style guidance
4. shadow and ethics framing

### 5. Information designer and data-visualization lead

Primary job:
Check whether the IA can actually produce a strong diagram, board, and chart system.

Key questions:

1. which claims need diagrams versus charts versus annotated boards
2. are the current planned visuals attached to real route jobs
3. do the chart suites answer one story question each
4. will the visual system help students decide faster and remember more

What this reviewer should approve:

1. diagram inventory placement
2. chart suite priorities
3. board and comparison formats
4. asset-to-route map

### 6. Accessibility and inclusive-design specialist

Primary job:
Check whether the IA is readable, navigable, and teachable for different learners before visual and audio production starts.

Key questions:

1. are the route and module patterns cognitively manageable
2. do audio, visuals, and charts all have transcript or descriptive pathways
3. is the system likely to overload first-generation or stressed students
4. are accessibility requirements stated as baseline production rules rather than cleanup tasks

What this reviewer should approve:

1. accessibility checklist
2. transcript and alt-text expectations
3. media accessibility rules
4. support and recovery pathways

### 7. Research librarian and citation editor

Primary job:
Check whether the IA leverages the research corpus rigorously and keeps source provenance visible.

Key questions:

1. does every major room have a source backbone
2. are quotes, examples, and claims traceable
3. are the research layers being surfaced in the right public form
4. is the system distinguishing between concept art, evidence, and authoritative source material

What this reviewer should approve:

1. research-to-room map
2. quote-rail discipline
3. evidence-card sourcing rules
4. source attribution for charts and exemplars

### 8. Media learning producer

Primary job:
Check whether the IA is specific enough to generate media packages, not just page copy.

Key questions:

1. which rooms deserve long-form audio
2. which ideas should become diagrams, narrated explainers, or comparison boards
3. is each major room scriptable for speech and visual teaching
4. can the current `eai` pipeline actually produce the proposed assets cleanly

What this reviewer should approve:

1. media package per route or room
2. audio slate and voice strategy
3. board and illustration priorities
4. production workflow assumptions

## Extended advisory panel

### 9. UX and student-journey reviewer

Primary job:
Test whether the intended user can move through the IA without author knowledge.

Key questions:

1. can a student find the right next move quickly
2. does the route language reduce or create confusion
3. where will students stall, skim, or bail out
4. does the path from atlas to studio to output feel natural

### 10. Technical implementation architect

Primary job:
Check whether the IA, asset plan, and content model can actually fit the current repo and release system.

Key questions:

1. does the route and asset structure fit the current experience model
2. are content and media storage assumptions realistic
3. can the selected-release system support the proposed experience without route chaos
4. are there production bottlenecks hidden inside the content plan

### 11. Faculty lead or subject-matter owner

Primary job:
Check whether the IA still reflects the educational intent of the program and the instructor voice.

Key questions:

1. does the site still reflect the real goals of the course and degree
2. is the seriousness level right
3. are historical and theoretical layers supporting action rather than delaying it
4. are the examples and artifacts credible for the actual student population

### 12. Career, employer-signal, or professional-practice reviewer

Primary job:
Check whether the visible outputs actually read as useful public proof outside the classroom.

Key questions:

1. do the artifact ladders produce externally legible work
2. are the publish and deployment layers realistic
3. do proof blocks and exemplars look credible to outside reviewers
4. is the program overselling what the artifacts can prove

## Recommended review order

### Pass 1: structural QA

Reviewers:

1. information architect and content strategist
2. instructional designer and curriculum architect
3. UX and student-journey reviewer

Goal:
Make sure the IA is structurally understandable and teachable before worrying about art direction.

### Pass 2: curation and doctrine QA

Reviewers:

1. curator and editorial experience director
2. brand and identity-systems strategist
3. research librarian and citation editor

Goal:
Make sure the IA uses the research corpus intelligently and that the atlas and archetype systems are not misframed.

### Pass 3: media and accessibility QA

Reviewers:

1. information designer and data-visualization lead
2. media learning producer
3. accessibility and inclusive-design specialist

Goal:
Make sure the IA can drive diagrams, charts, images, audio, and accessible media packages with real production value.

### Pass 4: reality and credibility QA

Reviewers:

1. technical implementation architect
2. faculty lead or subject-matter owner
3. career, employer-signal, or professional-practice reviewer

Goal:
Make sure the plan is buildable, pedagogically honest, and externally credible.

## Required sign-off outputs

Before major content and media production begins, the review process should produce:

1. a route and module approval note
2. a research-to-room approval note
3. an archetype and identity approval note
4. an asset-to-route approval note
5. an accessibility and transcript baseline note
6. an implementation-feasibility note

## Non-negotiable QA questions

These are the questions the full panel should be able to answer yes to.

1. does every top-level route have one clear job
2. can a student tell what to do next without author translation
3. is the atlas curated rather than archival
4. are archetypes being used as coherence constraints rather than flattering labels
5. does every major room have the right mix of panel, visual, evidence, and next move
6. can the research corpus actually feed the planned diagrams, charts, boards, and audio
7. are media assets attached to learning and decision jobs rather than decoration
8. is the current repo architecture sufficient to build the proposed experience
9. are accessibility and source-provenance rules explicit enough to govern production

## Minimum viable panel if staffing is tight

If you can only get a small review team, do not reduce below these five lenses.

1. information architect and content strategist
2. instructional designer and curriculum architect
3. curator and editorial experience director
4. research librarian and citation editor
5. information designer or media learning producer

That is the smallest group likely to keep the IA from becoming structurally vague, instructionally weak, visually decorative, or research-loose.

## Bottom line

The right QA panel for this IA is not just faculty plus a designer.

It needs at least these review intelligences:

1. structure
2. pedagogy
3. curation
4. identity and positioning
5. visual explanation
6. accessibility
7. source rigor
8. media production

That is the combination most likely to turn the IA into a dependable source document for building pages, diagrams, charts, long-form audio, and exemplar-led teaching assets from the research corpus.
