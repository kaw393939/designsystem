## Project goal

Build a world-class first site that helps students engineer their first product for themselves: a portfolio that becomes a clear public signal, a proof surface, and the beginning of a real deployment loop.

## What the site must do

1. Help students build a better portfolio using the identity system.
2. Give enough background and historical framing to make the work feel serious without slowing action.
3. Make archetype exploration reusable, visual, and concrete.
4. Make visual-design literacy teachable from Bauhaus and Swiss lineages through current product-design practice.
5. Make the psychology of attention, trust, and persuasion understandable without turning it into manipulative folklore.
6. Treat the portfolio as the first product the student ships.
7. Bridge from page improvement to real-world deployment in NYC-area and online AI communities.
8. Use multimedia deliberately so longer ideas can be heard, seen, compared, and remembered.

## Student-facing frame

The site should use a simple frame:

We have been here before.

Print lowered the cost of reproducing knowledge.
AI lowers the cost of producing and extending cognitive work.
When output gets cheaper, clarity, judgment, proof, and public action matter more.

The student's first product is not a startup pitch.
It is the clearest, most credible version of themselves in public.

## Experience pillars

### 1. Action first

The core path stays short:

Need -> Signal -> Style -> Proof -> Build -> Publish

### 2. Serious background, not a lecture pile

Historical and AI-era framing should support urgency and meaning, not turn the homepage into a theory dump.

### 3. Archetype clarity

Students need a gallery that makes identity legible through people, tone, imagery, layouts, proof style, and anti-patterns.

### 4. Visual design literacy

Students also need a second gallery family that teaches design lineages, not only personal identity.

They should learn how Bauhaus, Swiss, systems-modernist, postmodern, editorial, brutalist, and product-system traditions change hierarchy, trust, pace, and meaning.

### 5. Psychology literacy

Students need a third gallery family that explains attention, scanning, gaze, proof, trust, and ethical persuasion as practical design forces.

### 6. Visual richness

Use portraits, artifacts, diagrams, before-and-after page examples, and annotated proof blocks.
The site should feel closer to an editorial product or museum-grade teaching experience than to an LMS handout.

### 7. Real deployment

Students should leave with a page, a proof block, a short post, a meetup opener, and a follow-up script.

### 8. Multimedia depth

Longer content should not live only as walls of text.

The site should support audio companions, diagrams, comparison boards, charts, and narrated explainers for the deeper modules.

## World-class bar

The first site should feel:

1. historically grounded
2. visually intentional
3. useful on first read
4. concrete enough to produce artifacts
5. serious enough that students feel they are entering a real discipline

## Measures of success

1. students can complete a signal brief without instructor translation
2. students can choose a primary archetype with evidence instead of guessing
3. students leave the proof route with a reusable proof block
4. students can show their page to a real person within the same week they build it
5. students understand why the portfolio is part of their AI-era professional identity, not a side assignment

## Risks to avoid

1. letting the homepage become a theory page again
2. teaching archetypes as flattery instead of as coherence constraints
3. burying the main build path under optional labs or references
4. using historical framing as decoration instead of as a meaningful teaching frame
5. treating publish as optional enrichment
