## Purpose

Build a reusable brand-archetype gallery and exploration experience that helps students find a coherent primary signal, understand what that signal looks like in public, and avoid mixed-brand confusion.

This should be one of the flagship features of the site.

It should sit inside a broader gallery family with:

1. archetype and identity
2. visual design literacy
3. psychology, attention, and persuasion

## Why this matters

Students do not usually fail because they have no material.
They fail because the first read has no stable interpretive frame.

The gallery should solve three jobs:

1. teach archetypes as public meaning systems, not personality diagnoses
2. help students compare options visually and structurally
3. end with a usable decision, not endless browsing

## Book-informed gallery structure

The gallery should mirror the book's grouped logic instead of flattening all archetypes into one generic picker.

| Cluster | Archetypes | Teaching job |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Yearning for Paradise | Innocent, Explorer, Sage | fulfillment, discovery, meaning, guidance |
| Leaving a Thumbprint on the World | Hero, Outlaw, Magician | mastery, disruption, transformation |
| No Man or Woman is an Island | Regular Guy or Gal, Lover, Jester | belonging, intimacy, social connection |
| Providing Structure to the World | Caregiver, Creator, Ruler | care, making, order, stewardship |

Part 1 of the book should shape the gallery introduction because it explains why archetypes matter as a system for managing meaning.

Parts 6 and 7 should shape the closing sections because they introduce positioning, category essence, organizational congruence, and ethics.

## Required gallery modes

### 1. Browse mode

Students can scan all archetypes quickly with visual and language cues.

### 2. Compare mode

Students can put two or three archetypes side by side and see where voice, visuals, proof, and CTA diverge.

### 3. Decision mode

Students leave with:

1. primary archetype
2. optional subtle secondary archetype
3. one-sentence promise direction
4. proof style direction
5. visual lane direction

## Data model for each archetype

Each archetype entry should include:

1. name
2. one-line meaning
3. desire and fear
4. how it reads on the first screen
5. headline tone
6. visual cues
7. proof style
8. CTA style
9. anti-patterns
10. adjacent or commonly confused archetypes
11. historical or cultural figures
12. modern page or brand examples
13. student reflection prompts

## World-class standard

The gallery should not feel like a quiz.

It should feel like a visual editorial atlas with:

1. portraits and cultural figures
2. strong typographic identity
3. annotated page fragments
4. before-and-after signal corrections
5. direct guidance on what to keep and what to cut

## Multimedia layer

The archetype gallery should not rely only on reading.

It should support:

1. short audio introductions for each cluster
2. longer narrated explainers for each archetype family
3. transcripts and pull-quote cards for every audio asset
4. annotated visuals showing how an archetype changes page rhythm, proof style, and CTA tone
5. comparison boards that can become image, video, or slide assets later

The repo already has audio ingestion and transcription infrastructure through the book-transcript workflow.
The missing piece is a synthesis-to-audio publishing pass for the student-facing site.

## Teaching rules

1. archetypes are coherence devices, not hard science
2. one primary archetype is the beginner rule
3. subtle secondary traits are allowed only after the main signal is stable
4. every archetype view must tie back to audience, proof, and page job
5. the gallery must end in action, not in endless interpretation
