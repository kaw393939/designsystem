# Instructions to the Coding Agent: How to Think About Designing Courses

Your job is **not** to build an “online shell” around a class. Your job is to design a **coherent learning system** that works first for strong face-to-face teaching and then translates cleanly into online or hybrid delivery when needed.

The NJIT QA rubric should be treated as a **general course-quality framework**, not merely an online checklist. The rubric explicitly says it offers guidance for digital learning courses **as well as any mode of instruction**. 

## Core design doctrine

Design every course around these assumptions:

1. **The course must be legible to students from day one.**
   Students should immediately understand the purpose of the course, how to get started, how the course is organized, what they do each week, and when work is due. The syllabus, course schedule, and navigation structure should all agree with each other.

2. **Learning outcomes are the spine of the course.**
   Outcomes must be clear, measurable, and aligned with the official course description. Everything else—content, activities, discussions, labs, projects, and assessments—must trace back to those outcomes. 

3. **The course should be organized in meaningful weekly modules.**
   Even when the primary instruction is face-to-face, students need a weekly modular structure that tells them the topic, the purpose, the outcomes, the materials, the activities, and the deliverables. Each module should include an overview that explains how the pieces fit together. 

4. **The instructor must remain the primary voice.**
   Do not build courses that feel like a textbook dump, a link farm, or a sequence of disconnected uploads. The instructor should create and/or contextualize the materials so students understand why they are reading, watching, discussing, or building something. 

5. **Assignments must be scaffolded and aligned.**
   Major work should be broken into milestones, drafts, check-ins, reviews, or other staged progress points. Assignment pages must clearly explain purpose, expectations, format, success criteria, and the learning outcomes being practiced. 

6. **Interaction must be designed, not assumed.**
   In a classroom, some interaction happens naturally. Online, it must be intentionally designed. The course should create regular, meaningful contact among instructor, students, and content through discussion, feedback, announcements, Q&A spaces, critique, and guided collaboration.

7. **Accessibility and support are baseline quality, not extras.**
   Readable formatting, strong contrast, alt text, captions/transcripts, proper headings, descriptive hyperlinks, and clear student support resources should be treated as required components of good course design.

---

## The translation rule

Use this rule throughout your design work:

**What happens naturally in the room must be designed intentionally online.**

For example:

* In-person explanation becomes a written overview or short video.
* In-class discussion becomes a structured discussion board, collaborative annotation, or peer response activity.
* Live feedback becomes written/audio/video feedback in Canvas.
* Informal “ask me in class” becomes an “Ask the Instructor” forum.
* A lecture becomes segmented support material, not a raw hour-long recording.

## What you should build for every course

For each course, create a structure with these components:

### 1. Course entry layer

Build a clear opening experience that includes:

* a welcome/start-here page
* course purpose and relevance
* instructor presence/self-introduction
* syllabus
* schedule with consistent due dates
* explanation of how the course works week to week

### 2. Outcome layer

For the full course and for each module:

* define measurable learning outcomes
* ensure they align with catalog expectations
* ensure all outcomes are actually assessed somewhere in the course 

### 3. Weekly module layer

Each module should contain:

* module title/topic
* module overview
* module outcomes
* required materials
* in-class activity plan
* online support elements
* assessments/deliverables
* due dates and instructions presented consistently from week to week 

### 4. Instructional materials layer

Materials should be:

* purposeful
* connected to outcomes
* contextualized by the instructor
* balanced across media types
* segmented when delivered asynchronously, usually into shorter chunks rather than long recordings 

### 5. Assessment layer

Design a mix of:

* formative practice
* scaffolded milestones
* authentic assessments such as projects, presentations, case studies, demonstrations, critiques, or applied work
* digital submission pathways through Canvas rather than email-based grading

### 6. Interaction and feedback layer

Every course should specify:

* how students interact with the instructor
* how students interact with one another
* how and when students receive meaningful feedback
* response-time expectations
* office hours and announcement rhythm
* a public course Q&A mechanism when appropriate

### 7. Accessibility and support layer

Every course should include:

* accessible formatting and structure
* captions/transcripts for media
* alt text for images
* descriptive linked text
* student support / technology help resources
* tool guidance for course technologies

## How to handle face-to-face primary instruction

Assume the classroom is the primary site of teaching, but the LMS is the **instructional operating system** that gives the course continuity, clarity, and resilience.

That means:

* The classroom is where discussion, demonstration, coaching, critique, and collective energy happen.
* Canvas is where structure, instructions, materials, deadlines, submission, reflection, and continuity live.
* The LMS should never feel like an afterthought. It should make the in-person course easier to follow, easier to recover, and easier to scale.

Do **not** simply copy online-course mechanics into face-to-face teaching. Instead, translate them intelligently:

* Replace mandatory weekly discussion boards with in-class discussion when the room can support it.
* Preserve the intellectual function of the discussion by adding short written reflections, synthesis posts, or follow-up prompts only where they add value.
* Use online tools to extend, prepare, document, and reinforce face-to-face learning rather than duplicate it.

---

## How to handle online modifications

When adapting a face-to-face course for online or partially online delivery:

1. Add stronger orientation and navigation.
2. Make module overviews more explicit.
3. Convert live explanation into concise asynchronous supports.
4. Replace spontaneous interaction with planned interaction.
5. Increase visibility of instructor presence through announcements, feedback, and structured communication.
6. Ensure every major task has clear written instructions and digital submission flow.

---

## What I want from you as deliverables

For each course, produce:

1. **A course architecture document**
   Explains the instructional philosophy, weekly rhythm, and how the course works across face-to-face and online contexts.

2. **An outcomes map**
   Shows course outcomes, module outcomes, and where each outcome is taught and assessed.

3. **A module template**
   A reusable weekly structure that can be applied consistently across the semester.

4. **An assessment scaffold**
   Shows how low-stakes work builds toward higher-stakes work.

5. **An interaction plan**
   Explains where discussion, feedback, announcements, Q&A, and instructor presence occur.

6. **An accessibility/support checklist**
   Ensures the course materials and LMS structure meet baseline readability, accessibility, and support expectations.

---

## Your quality bar

A course is not complete unless:

* students can tell what to do each week without confusion
* every major activity clearly supports learning outcomes
* the modules are consistent and readable
* the instructor’s voice is visible throughout the course
* assessments are scaffolded rather than dumped on students
* interaction is intentionally designed
* accessibility is built in from the start
* the online layer strengthens the face-to-face course instead of merely archiving files

---

## Final operating principle

Think like an instructional architect, not a content uploader.

You are designing a course so that:

* students understand the path,
* the instructor’s expertise is felt,
* learning activities clearly build toward meaningful performance,
* and the course can flex between face-to-face, hybrid, and online without losing coherence.
