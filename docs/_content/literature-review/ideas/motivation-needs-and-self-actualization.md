# Motivation, Needs, and Self-Actualization

## Why this topic matters here

The identity portfolio system starts with need, not features.

That choice is not cosmetic. It determines whether a portfolio reads like a pile of tasks or like a response to something a real person actually cares about.

## What the internal research argues

Across `identity-system-core.md`, `mysystem.md`, and `identity.md`, the project makes four claims:

1. human need is the starting point for signal design
2. Maslow is useful if treated as a map, not a rigid staircase
3. self-actualization matters, but only when kept connected to service and reality
4. student motivation is shaped by agency, competence, and belonging, not by information alone

## What the literature supports

### Maslow remains useful, but not as a rigid ladder

Maslow’s 1943 paper is still valuable because it organizes motivation around basic needs and higher-order growth, but the paper itself is more flexible than the simplified pyramid that later became popular.

Two lines from the original paper matter for this project:

- “Typically an act has more than one motivation.”
- “The degree of fixity of the hierarchy of basic needs ... is not nearly as rigid as we may have implied.”

Those lines support the project’s best current framing: use Maslow to identify the need field a page is speaking to, not to claim that all people move through one universal sequence.

### Later critique strengthens the project’s current caveat

Wahba and Bridwell’s 1976 review is important because it helps prevent the project from overstating Maslow. The main takeaway is not that need categories are useless. It is that the strict sequence claim is not strongly supported.

Tay and Diener’s global study is also useful because it suggests that multiple needs relate to well-being around the world without acting like a single locked staircase.

### Self-Determination Theory is especially useful for the educational side

Ryan and Deci’s framework is a better fit for the project’s pedagogical ambitions.

The SDT site defines the theory this way:

> “Self-Determination Theory (SDT) represents a broad framework for understanding why we do what we do…and what leads to flourishing.”

Its strongest contribution here is the autonomy, competence, and relatedness triad.

That triad translates directly into course design:

1. autonomy: students need meaningful choice and authorship
2. competence: students need visible progress and skill feedback
3. relatedness: students need social connection, audience, and support

### Kenrick’s update is useful because it restores context and trade-offs

Kenrick and colleagues update Maslow by treating motives as more dynamic, evolutionary, and context-sensitive. This is useful because the portfolio system is not dealing with a timeless abstract person. It is dealing with students in unstable markets, AI-driven pressure, and changing life stages.

## What this means for the project

### For the portfolio system

1. every page should speak to a need before it lists capabilities
2. every portfolio should identify whether it is primarily answering safety, belonging, esteem, growth, or contribution needs
3. no page should assume that prestige language alone creates motivation

### For the course design

1. each major step should support autonomy, competence, and relatedness
2. students should leave each page with a visible artifact so competence feels real
3. publishing and feedback matter because motivation is social, not only internal

## Cautions

1. do not teach Maslow as settled universal law
2. do not confuse self-actualization with self-display
3. do not treat “purpose” language as a substitute for clarity or market fit
4. do not build a course that assumes students already have stable autonomy, attention, or emotional capacity

## Working doctrine

The strongest synthesis for this project is:

Need is real, layered, and contextual. Motivation improves when people feel more choice, more growing competence, and more connection. A portfolio should therefore translate human need into a clear signal and give the student enough agency and proof to keep moving.
